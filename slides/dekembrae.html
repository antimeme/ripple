Dekembræ

Dekembræ is a role playing game system intended to support table top and computer games with a variety of genres.

Goals

Role playing games have been around a long time but most have serious problems that work against story telling and exciting combat. Dekembræ seeks to address these and present a more robust system.

Rolling Dice for Abilities

In the earliest role playing games players rolled dice to determine character abilities. This can be exciting but players lose the power to construct the kinds of characters they want to play. Dice also make it difficult to know whether a player cheated. Finally, players can have characters with substantial differences in power through no virtue or fault of their own.

Character Classes

Most role playing games make players choose a character class as one of the earliest steps. This simplifies the game for players, which is helpful. However, in most games this constrains the player choices so much that the designers feel compelled to add a multi-class system -- usually a cumbersome one. Dekembræ seeks to reverse this by presenting a flexible base system with optional character classes to simplify the game when desired.

Escalating Physical Toughness

As characters gain experience, they usually gain some form of hit points. This makes experienced characters more difficult to kill but despite sometimes explicit explanations in source books, most game masters interpret this as a kind of physical resistance to damage. This strains the story telling as characters endure increasingly absurd amounts of physical punishment.

Frequent Fumbles

Most role playing games use flat probability distributions, in contrast to real world systems where bell curves are far more common. This creates strange situations, such as experienced characters who fumble even basic tasks one time in twenty.

Awkward Balancing Mechanics

Games in which characters can use powerful systems like fantasy magic must balance these abilities so that characters without such abilities still have important roles to play. Unfortunately many role playing systems use implausible and poorly constructed balancing mechanisms, such as limiting powers to a small number of uses per day. This strains credibility and undermines story telling when there are better mechanic alternatives available.

Experience Points for Kills

The earliest role playing games rewareded characters with experience points for killing monsters. This is a simple and direct mechanic, but it creates a structural incentive to solve as many problems as possible with violence. Players who consider bribing or sneaking past enemies are forced to consider the loss of game rewards that have no direct role in the story.

Deterministic Character Speed

In some role playing games characters have a fixed speed at which they can move. This makes chase scenes predictable and dull.

Goals

  • Bell curve distributions
  • Deterministic character creation
  • Flexible character creation
  • Story friendly mechancis
  • Plausible combat that models:
    • Striking (with or without weapons)
    • Speed and Reach
    • Dodging, Parrying and Binding
    • Cover and Concealment
    • Shields and Armor
    • Stamina and Wounds
  • Universal system for many genres

Character Abilities

  • Brawn: physical toughness and strength
  • Agility: coordination and flexibility
  • Perception: managing complex sensory input
  • Intellect: abstract problem solving
  • Will: perserverance and determination

Characters start with a score of 3 in all abilities. Each can be increased by one point at a cost equal to the current value plus one. A score of 4 for an ability is average. Heroic characters get 50 points to spend. Other kinds of characters may get fewer.

Skill Rolls

Characters make skill rolls by rolling dice. The number of dice depend on the action but is usually equal to the number of ranks the character has in a single skill. Before a skill role is made a character sometimes has the option to sacrifice ranks to activate special abilities. At most nine dice may be rolled for any skill roll. Any dice beyond nine are dropped and three points for each are added to the result. Players can choose to replace any dice above five with the value four instead.

For example, a character with 10 ranks in a skill can choose to roll 9 dice and add (1 × 3) = 3 or can choose to roll as few as 5 dice, adding (4 × 4) + (1 × 3) = 16 + 3 = 19.

The remaining dice are resolved by rolling according to the d10* process. Roll a ten sided die. Results of 0 through 8 are added at their face value. Results of 9 are converted to 9 plus the result of another d10* process.

For example, suppose a character has 8 ranks in a skill. She chooses to use a special ability that costs one rank, leaving 7 ranks. She decides to convert one die to a 4 and roll the remaining six. The face value of the dice are: 5, 3, 9, 2, 7. Since one of the dice was a 9 she rolls one more, which comes up 0. The result of the skill role is 4 + 5 + 3 + 9 + 2 + 7 + 0 = 30. This is slightly below the expected value 7 dice, which would be 35. The character was a little unlucky.

Combat

Combat can take place either in a discrete or continuous context. Discrete combat is divided into five second rounds and is well suited for table top play. Continuous combat is well suited for real-time computer games.

At any moment each character has a stamina and fatigue score. Stamina can never be greater than brawn times will minus fatigue. At the start of each round, if a character has not engaged in any strenuous activity one twenty fifth of maximum stamina is restored. This is not rounded. Stamina values less than one are effectively zero.

Status

At any moment a character is in one of the following states:

  • Helpless/Unconscious: no passive dodge, no active dodge
  • Casual/Bound: no passive dodge, active dodge
  • Ready: passive dodge, active dodge

Attacking

Attacks are resolved with the following system. The attacker begins with the number of ranks in a base skill such as strike, throw or aim. To this the attacker adds any ranks in an category skill such as blades or polearms. Then the attacker adds specific skills such as dagger or halbard. The total number is the number of ranks available. This is used to make a skill roll which determines the attack score.

Each possible weapon attack has a difficulty value. If the attack score is less than this value then the attack was an embarrassing fumble. Otherwise, subtract the difficulty from the attack score and continue.

When a defender is conscious and aware of the attacker, compare the current attack score to the agility of the defender. If the attack score is smaller, the defender steps aside effortlessly. Otherwise subtract the passive dodge score and continue.

If the attack score is still more than zero and the defender is conscious and able to move, the defender active defense must be resolved. Active defense uses stamina to reduce the attack score. Each point of stamina can reduce the attack by the defender agility. If the defender has enough stamina to reduce the attack to zero or below then that many points are spent and the attack is narrowly avoided. If not, the defender loses only a single point of stamina and the attack continues.

Note that even if the attack is avoided in this way, the weapon still contacts the defender and may draw blood unless completely absorbed by defender armor. A defender in defensive mode adds another five points to the attack score and spends enough stamina to reduce this to zero if possible. This may be important if effects like poison are in play.

Determine the region in which the attack lands. The probability of the attack affecting a region is proportional to it's weight relative to the sum of all region weights. If the attack score is lower than the armor coverage for that region then the attack strikes the armor, which will absorb some portion of the damage. If not the attacker has found a gap in the armor and the defender takes full damage from the weapon.

Attack options:

  • Parry: use weapon ranks in place of dodge skill, stamina cost limited to damage dice on attacking weapon
  • Threatening attack: spend one rank, successful attack is not resolved until defender tries to move or attack (attacker can opt to convert to training attack).
  • Training attack: spend one rank, successful does no damage if attacker can succeed on a second attack role against difficulty and passive defense
  • Region attack: spend two ranks, any successful attack hits the selected region.
  • Disarm: spend one rank plus a number of additional ranks, on successful attack against defender passive defense, defender must succeed on attack role against attackers passive dodge minus additional ranks spent by attacker.
  • Off Hand: characters have a dominant hand, using the other doubles difficulty and does not allow parry to add to first point of stamina

Parry

On their turn a character may choose to enter parry mode. When a character is in parry mode two things change. First, instead of adding dodge skill to agility for reducing attack scores, the total ranks the character has with one of the weapons in hand is used instead. Active defense can cost no more than the number of damage dice done by the attacking weapon.

Wounds

Wounds come in three forms: minor, serious and critical. The wounded character must make a brawn roll. A wound less than a fifth of this roll (rounded up) is a minor wound. A wound less than this roll is serious and reduces stamina by one point at the start of each round until treated. A wound greater than this is critical and reduces stamina by two points per round until treated. An arm or leg with a critical wound or two serious wounds cannot be used until treated. A critical wound to the body renders the character helpless. A critical wound or two serious wounds to the head renders the character unconscious.

A character with a serious leg wound suffers a movement penalty. A character with a critical leg wound or two serious wounds to the same leg can't stand up and has no passive defense. A character with a serious arm wound must treat that arm as an off hand. A character with two serious wounds or one critical wound to an arm cannot use that arm at all.

Critical wounds leave permanent scars and may instantly kill non-player characters. A critical wound to the body or head will kill a character in a number of rounds equal to brawn ranks unless the character is stabilized. Personality

Non-player characters have a personality which is described by five variables, each of which has one of five values:

  • Openness
  • Conscientiousness
  • Extraversion
  • Agreeableness
  • Neuroticism

Each variable has one of the following values:

  1. Imperceptible (0.1)
  2. Limited (0.25)
  3. Typical (0.3)
  4. Pronounced (0.25)
  5. Extreme (0.1)

There are 5 x 5 x 5 x 5 x 5 = 3,125 personality possibilities, though some are more common than others.
